About me

I wrote my first ‘Hello World’ in the late ‘80s on a Tandy 1000. My whole family became obsessed. I remember my mom actually having to put a cap on the number of those little ‘cookbook’ style learn-to-code books that we could check out from the library, and the bargains we made to try and get a few extra. Since then, technology and the need to teach myself have been constant threads in my life.

We moved around a lot, and a series of small rural schools without internet and fewer than a dozen computers in the building made it difficult to find opportunities to learn more. I still learned what I could. I would type assignments when I could, I would figure out how to put small algorithms together to ‘check’ my math homework, and I would use our barely-there dial-up connection to try and learn more.

In the Marine Corps I worked as a fire support man in an artillery company. I was one of two that could get the computer-based indirect fire coordination system to work, took every opportunity to get the range-finding and laser designating systems out to the field, helped troubleshoot communications equipment, and even digitized the scheduled calls for fire for the larger joint exercises.

During my undergraduate studies I often found technical work in the summer. I worked two summers on databases, mainly doing data entry the first summer, but the second summer putting a new database together. In that second year I worked for a bank for the first time, for a commercial loans department about which I knew nothing, building a database with all the related forms and reports which I had never studied or attempted. In the space of a few months, while splitting my time between the commercial loans department and filling in for a collections clerk that left unexpectedly, I taught myself what I needed to know to put it all together. I learned enough about how databases worked, built the table structures and relationships, split off the front end, built the forms for data entry, built the reports they needed, and built and scheduled monthly client letters. I did all this without taking a class, without having a more experienced person to consult, and without even an existing database to model mind from.

In grad school I started out in a grant program to deliver professional development to math teachers in rural Michigan. The grant funds were cut back and the renewal stipulated that they needed to shift to distance learning, and they had no idea how to proceed. I started by reviewing existing platforms to see if there was something in place we could implement. Due to the technology available at the time, and the state of internet access in rural areas, an online only solution was not reasonable. We ended up putting together a mix of a few technologies that we used to create modules that could distribute using physical media. We had to make sure it ran on a variety of equipment and that it was user-friendly for the local teacher-leaders that would deliver the PD to small groups at the schools. This also allowed for the opportunity of having professors design learning opportunities for the teachers, but required us to coordinate and assist the faculty members in designing their modules. As there were only four of us on the grant – a professor emeriti with very limited knowledge of the technology, an administrative assistant, a curriculum focused graduate assistant, and myself – the bulk of the work coordinating with faculty, facilitating their work, and assisting with the more technical aspects fell to me.

In working with the faculty members from a wide range of specialties, I wound up being exposed to a variety of different disciplines and the technologies specific to them. In particular I ended up with a solid background in R, SPSS, MatLab, and Maple before they came up in the graduate curriculum. With my background I was able to excel in the computational mathematics, probability, and statistics coursework and was asked to help in a separate grant program working on the assessment team collecting and analyzing implementation data on a textbook that was being developed.

As an Assistant Professor of Mathematics and Mathematics Education at the University of New Haven (UNH) I was asked to teach the Discreet Mathematics courses for Computer Science majors due to my background in graph theory, probability/statistics, propositional logic, and computer programming. In teaching that class I learned a fair bit of python to use in student exercises, as that is what they were using in the concurrently enrolled CSCI course. I also ended up teaching in the Digital Media Literacy graduate program for one summer.

Since then I have taught myself C# and the Unity engine in order to help with a course at Wilbur Cross and to work on hobby projects. I taught myself Swift and used that with XCode to begin work on a classroom management app, and shifted to working on Java when I became serious about making the career change to software development. I learned JavaScript/HTML/CSS to put together my website (which originally included a MongoDB NoSQL database, but were stripped out when I chose to host on AWS’s S3 environment), which also uses Bootstrap and React to make the code more modular and present a user-friendly environment on various screen sizes.

Current Project

Right now I am working on learning ASP.net Core. I am going to use a sample database that was a part of a TSQL course to make a mock-eCommerce website.

In terms of getting the ‘bones’ of it all set up, it has been fairly straightforward. EF Core makes connecting the back end and front end fairly straightforward. I am using a database with only five tables. I wrote the data model classes, and then simply scaffolded the CRUD pages.

I have been reading about how some people prefer to put the services in a separate class’ static method then call that in ConfigureServices (and another for the Configure method) to be ready for use as Dependency Injections (DIs). I definitely see how this would be advantageous in larger projects. The startup file gets a little long and crowded if you end up using a large number of services, and it gives you separate spaces that can focus on single purposes. It also makes it so that you can’t inadvertently mess up your services if you’re working on the HTTP request pipeline. My thought is that I will do this for ConfigureServices, but leave Configure’s code in Setup.cs. I very much like the idea of having all the services in their own dedicated area, but I don’t know enough about the overhead in moving the HTTP request pipeline out of that file, and I need to do a little more studying before I’m comfortable making a decision to move it.

I have a lot to do with the scaffolded CRUD pages. They are a nice starting point, but there are several things that could certainly be done better. For instance, setting up a new customer and inputting their address should all be one page, but the scaffolding process put those separately. Things like that need to be addressed. It is giving me a lot of practice with dependency injections and the async/wait pattern, though, which is great.
